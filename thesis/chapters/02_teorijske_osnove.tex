\section{Theoretical Background and Literature Review}

\subsection{Why intermarket dependency matters}

How assets move together sits at the centre of financial theory: diversification, contagion, hedging, and portfolio risk all hinge on it. The textbook mean-variance framework treats that co-movement as a single constant correlation, but decades of evidence show the static picture does not hold. Real correlations drift over time, react more violently to bad news than to good, and spike during market downturns---exactly when diversification is supposed to help most \cite{longin2001}. Forecasting \emph{time-varying dependence} is therefore not an academic luxury but a practical necessity for managing risk.

All of this matters even more when one side of the pair is a cryptocurrency. Bitcoin trades around the clock on a globally accessible market, reacts quickly to swings in sentiment and funding liquidity, and answers to a tangle of competing narratives at once: an inflation hedge to some, a speculative bet to others, a barometer of risk appetite, a hostage to regulation. Because so many forces pull on it, Bitcoin never settles into a single, stable role in a portfolio. Depending on where we are in the business and credit cycle, it can behave like a high-beta growth stock, a liquidity-sensitive risk proxy, or an idiosyncratic sentiment gauge. That instability is exactly what makes its link to conventional assets a natural candidate for a time-varying treatment.

\subsection{Cryptocurrencies and conventional assets}

Research at the intersection of cryptocurrency and conventional markets has grown quickly over the past decade, and it splits into a few broad lines. The first treats Bitcoin on its own terms, mapping its return distribution, volatility clustering, tail risk, and weak-form efficiency. The second asks the portfolio question: does Bitcoin actually diversify a book of equities, gold, oil, or major currencies? The third---the one closest to this thesis---studies connectedness, contagion, and the spillover of returns and volatility across markets, with a particular eye on crises and stress episodes.

One result keeps coming back: the relationship is unstable \cite{bouri2017hedge, corbet2018exploring}. In calm markets, Bitcoin's link to broad equity indices is weak or inconsistent in direction; in pronounced risk-on or risk-off episodes, it tightens sharply \cite{ji2018network, kristoufek2015main}. Precious metals co-move with Bitcoin less, and less reliably \cite{dyhrberg2016bitcoin, klein2018bitcoin}, which fits their different investor base and long-standing safe-haven role. Currencies add a further angle, standing in for global funding liquidity \cite{corbet2018exploring}.

These patterns are exactly why this thesis models dependence as a moving target rather than a fixed number. A single full-sample correlation would average away the very behaviour of interest. Treating dependence as a time-varying process instead leaves room for slow regime drift, sudden breaks, and the fact that different asset pairs and window lengths shift at different speeds and in different directions.

\subsection{Rolling correlation as a forecasting target}

The rolling window correlation estimator is conceptually transparent and computationally tractable. For two return series $\{r_{1,t}\}$ and $\{r_{2,t}\}$, the rolling correlation at time $t$ over a window of length $w$ is defined as
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\rho}_{t}^{(w)} = \frac{\sum_{s=t-w+1}^{t}(r_{1,s} - \bar{r}_{1,t})(r_{2,s} - \bar{r}_{2,t})}
    {\sqrt{\sum_{s=t-w+1}^{t}(r_{1,s} - \bar{r}_{1,t})^{2}} \cdot
     \sqrt{\sum_{s=t-w+1}^{t}(r_{2,s} - \bar{r}_{2,t})^{2}}},
\end{equation}
where $\bar{r}_{k,t}$ denotes the in-window mean of series $k$. Traversing this estimator across all $t$ yields a time series $\{\hat{\rho}_{t}^{(w)}\}$ that characterizes the temporal evolution of intermarket co-movement.

As a forecasting target, rolling correlation has several things going for it. It is easy to read: positive values mean the two assets move together, negative values mean one hedges the other. It is comparable across asset pairs and window lengths, which makes a systematic multi-asset study possible. And it keeps the focus on how market structure is changing rather than on the level of any single return series.

Its main complication is strong serial persistence. Because $\hat{\rho}_{t}^{(w)}$ is built from overlapping windows, consecutive estimates share $w-1$ of their observations, so the series is heavily autocorrelated by construction. That persistence is likely to dominate any forecast and may leave complex models with little to add over a simple autoregression. Rather than fight this, the thesis treats it as a substantive finding about the dependency process in its own right.

To improve the statistical behavior of the target, the thesis applies the Fisher $z$-transformation:
\begin{equation}
    z_{t}^{(w)} = \frac{1}{2}\ln\!\left(\frac{1 + \hat{\rho}_{t}^{(w)}}{1 - \hat{\rho}_{t}^{(w)}}\right),
\end{equation}
which stretches the bounded values $\hat{\rho} \in (-1,1)$ onto the whole real line and gives them a sampling distribution much closer to Gaussian. It is standard practice for correlation-like quantities and tames the variance that otherwise blows up near $\pm 1$.

The main alternative is a copula-based approach \cite{patton2006copula}, which models the marginals and the dependence structure separately and so captures tail dependence and nonlinear co-movement that Pearson correlation simply cannot see. This thesis stays with rolling Pearson correlation for four reasons: (i) it is directly interpretable for risk managers; (ii) it is cheap enough to recompute at every step of the walk-forward loop; (iii) it lines up with the DCC-GARCH benchmark, whose output is exactly the conditional correlation matrix $R_t$ \cite{engle2001theoretical}; and (iv) the Fisher $z$-transformation already handles the boundary behaviour. The Pearson restriction is a genuine limitation, and copula extensions are the natural next step.

\subsection{Econometric benchmark: DCC-GARCH}

The Dynamic Conditional Correlation GARCH model, introduced by \textcite{engle2002}, provides the classical econometric benchmark for the present study. The DCC-GARCH framework decomposes the conditional covariance matrix $H_t$ of a vector of returns $\mathbf{r}_t$ according to
\begin{equation}
    H_t = D_t R_t D_t,
\end{equation}
where $D_t = \operatorname{diag}(h_{1,t}^{1/2}, h_{2,t}^{1/2})$ is a diagonal matrix of conditional standard deviations and $R_t$ is the time-varying conditional correlation matrix. Each conditional variance $h_{k,t}$ follows a univariate GARCH(1,1) process,
\begin{equation}
    h_{k,t} = \omega_k + \alpha_k \varepsilon_{k,t-1}^{2} + \beta_k h_{k,t-1},
\end{equation}
where $\varepsilon_{k,t} = r_{k,t}/h_{k,t}^{1/2}$ is the standardized residual. The standardized residuals $\tilde{\varepsilon}_t = D_t^{-1} r_t$ are then used to update the pseudo-correlation matrix $Q_t$ via
\begin{equation}
    Q_t = (1 - a - b)\bar{Q} + a\,\tilde{\varepsilon}_{t-1}\tilde{\varepsilon}_{t-1}^{\top} + b\, Q_{t-1},
\end{equation}
where $\bar{Q}$ is the unconditional covariance of standardized residuals, and the scalar parameters $a \geq 0$, $b \geq 0$, $a + b < 1$ govern the speed of mean reversion and the persistence of conditional correlation. The conditional correlation matrix is recovered as
\begin{equation}
    R_t = \operatorname{diag}(Q_t)^{-1/2}\, Q_t\, \operatorname{diag}(Q_t)^{-1/2}.
\end{equation}

DCC-GARCH is a natural benchmark here: it models time-varying co-movement under exactly the heteroskedastic conditions that crypto and equity returns both display. Its weakness is the flip side of that structure---a fixed parametric form has limited room to adapt to nonlinearities or abrupt breaks. There is also a subtler trap: fitting the model on the full sample before reading off ``out-of-sample'' predictions quietly leaks future information. To rule that out, the thesis re-estimates DCC-GARCH on an expanding window at every evaluation step, exactly as it does for the machine-learning models.

\subsection{Machine-learning approaches to forecasting}

Machine learning earns its place here not because it is guaranteed to win, but because it can pick up nonlinearities, threshold effects, and interactions that are awkward to write down as a parametric model. The thesis pits these methods against both the DCC-GARCH benchmark and plain persistence baselines, with two aims: to see where their strengths actually lie, and to pin down the conditions under which they add anything at all.

\subsubsection{The HAR model and persistence-based forecasting}

The Heterogeneous Autoregressive (HAR) model, introduced by \textcite{corsi2009har}, provides a natural benchmark for forecasting persistent financial time series. The HAR model approximates long-memory dynamics by summing three autoregressive components operating at daily, weekly, and monthly horizons:
\begin{equation}
    z_t = \phi_0 + \phi_d z_{t-1} + \phi_w \bar{z}_{t-5:t-1}
          + \phi_m \bar{z}_{t-22:t-1} + \varepsilon_t,
\end{equation}
where $\bar{z}_{t-k:t-1}$ is the arithmetic mean of $z$ over the preceding $k$ periods. The fit to rolling-correlation targets is almost too neat: those targets already carry a moving-average smoothing from their overlapping windows, which is the very structure HAR is built to exploit \cite{andersen2003realized}. In practice, HAR here is just a restricted OLS on three regressors---the latest value (daily), the 5-day mean (weekly), and the 22-day mean (monthly)---evaluated under the same walk-forward protocol as everything else.

\subsubsection{Regularized linear models}

Regularized linear models, including Ridge regression and Elastic Net, represent strong baselines in structured tabular forecasting settings. Ridge regression imposes an $\ell_2$ penalty on the coefficient vector, shrinking estimates toward zero to reduce estimation variance without introducing sparsity. Elastic Net combines $\ell_1$ and $\ell_2$ regularization:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = \operatorname*{arg\,min}_{\boldsymbol{\beta}} \left\{
        \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i - \mathbf{x}_i^{\top}\boldsymbol{\beta})^2
        + \lambda \left[(1-\alpha)\|\boldsymbol{\beta}\|_2^2 + \alpha\|\boldsymbol{\beta}\|_1\right]
    \right\},
\end{equation}
where $\lambda > 0$ sets the overall strength of the penalty and $\alpha \in [0,1]$ splits it between the two terms \cite{zou2005}. The $\ell_1$ part drives some coefficients to zero, doing implicit variable selection---handy when many lagged predictors are weak or redundant. That suits the feature set used here, which is full of overlapping transformations of correlated return and volatility series.

Linear though they are, these models often hold their own on smooth, persistence-driven problems. When the signal-to-noise ratio is low and the single most useful feature is just a lag of the target, the bias from assuming linearity is usually a price worth paying for the variance that regularization buys back.

\subsubsection{Tree-based ensemble models}

Random Forests construct an ensemble of decision trees, each trained on a bootstrapped subsample and a random subset of features, and aggregate their predictions to reduce variance and improve generalization \cite{breiman2001}. Gradient Boosting Machines instead build trees sequentially, with each new tree fitted to the pseudo-residuals of the current ensemble \cite{friedman2001}. XGBoost extends this boosting framework with second-order gradient approximations, $\ell_1$ and $\ell_2$ regularization terms on tree structure, and computational optimizations that facilitate large-scale application \cite{chen2016}.

Tree ensembles are obvious candidates because the link between crypto features and intermarket dependence may well be nonlinear and full of interactions. How much Bitcoin's realized volatility tells you about future equity dependence, for example, might depend on whether correlation is already running high. Trees can find and use that kind of conditional pattern on their own, without the analyst spelling out the interactions in advance.

None of this makes trees a sure bet for one-step-ahead forecasts of a highly persistent target. When the target is governed almost entirely by its last value, the extra flexibility may buy little signal while paying a real variance penalty. That tension is one of the main reasons the thesis insists on a disciplined walk-forward comparison rather than a single headline number.

\subsection{Walk-forward evaluation and information leakage}

Time order is not negotiable in financial forecasting, and it dictates how the evaluation must be set up. A random train-test split is simply not allowed: it scrambles the causal ordering and lets a model trained on period $t + k$ ``predict'' period $t$. The only honest design is one in which every forecast uses nothing but information available at or before its own origin.

So the thesis walks forward: train on an expanding history, make a one-step-ahead prediction, extend the window by one day, and repeat. To keep the cost manageable, models are refit at regular intervals rather than on every single step---a compromise that also mirrors how a real forecasting system would actually run. Either way, there is no path for future information to sneak in.

The protocol matters as much as the choice of model. A complex model with a hidden leak can look like it crushes a simpler, honestly evaluated rival, and the whole comparison is then worthless. Since the point of the thesis is to reach conclusions that hold up, a clean evaluation is treated as non-negotiable.

\subsection{Model comparison and the Diebold--Mariano test}

Ranking models by their average out-of-sample error is informative but not conclusive: it says nothing about whether a gap is real or just sampling noise. The Diebold--Mariano test closes that gap, formally testing the null that two forecasts are equally accurate \cite{diebold1995}.

Let $e_{1,t}$ and $e_{2,t}$ denote the forecast errors of models 1 and 2 at time $t$, and let $d_t = g(e_{1,t}) - g(e_{2,t})$ denote the loss differential under a loss function $g(\cdot)$ (typically squared or absolute error). The DM test statistic is
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{DM} = \frac{\bar{d}}{\sqrt{\hat{V}(\bar{d})\,/\, T}} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1),
\end{equation}
where $\bar{d} = T^{-1}\sum_{t=1}^{T} d_t$ is the mean loss differential, $\hat{V}(\bar{d})$ is a heteroskedasticity- and autocorrelation-consistent (HAC) variance estimator, and $T$ is the number of evaluation periods. Under the null of equal predictive accuracy, the statistic is asymptotically standard normal.

Here the DM test does two jobs. First, it checks whether the best machine-learning setup genuinely beats the leakage-safe DCC-GARCH benchmark. Second, it checks whether any edge over a plain autoregression or naive persistence baseline survives once sampling noise is accounted for. The second test is the more revealing one: if a model cannot significantly beat AR(1), then most of the forecastability lives in the target's own persistence, not in anything the cross-asset features have learned.

\subsection{From dependency forecasting to investor signaling}

Forecasting rolling correlation accurately would already be a complete academic exercise. But the thesis set out with a practical motive, and that calls for one more step: turning the forecasts into something an investor can act on, framed around the risk-management decision itself.

That step is a second-stage signal layer. Instead of guessing tomorrow's return direction, it estimates the probability that tomorrow lands in a \emph{stress regime}---a day of elevated downside risk in the conventional asset under study. This is much closer to how risk management actually works: the goal is not to call every wiggle, but to flag deteriorating conditions early enough to act on them.

Keeping the two stages separate keeps the design honest. Lower forecast error and genuine usefulness for portfolio decisions are not the same thing, and blurring them is an easy way to oversell a result. The two-layer structure lets the thesis report on each dimension on its own terms.

\subsection{Positioning of the present thesis}

The thesis sits where financial econometrics, machine learning, and applied risk analysis meet. It makes no claim that crypto data solves market timing. The claim is narrower, and more defensible: Bitcoin's price dynamics carry statistically detectable information about how intermarket dependence evolves, and that information can power a modest early-warning signal for conventional markets.

Its main contribution is to establish that dynamic intermarket dependence is forecastable, and to compare persistence baselines, machine-learning models, and DCC-GARCH on a level walk-forward footing. The investor signal layer pushes the work in a practical direction without claiming more than the evidence will bear.
