\section{Empirical Results}

\subsection{Overview of the empirical output}
\label{sec:results_overview}

The pipeline leaves behind a full, internally consistent trail of evidence: out-of-sample metrics, model rankings, Diebold--Mariano tests, signal-layer classification statistics, the prediction series themselves, and diagnostic figures. That breadth is deliberate. The empirical case here does not hang on a single table; it rests on whether the same story holds up from several angles at once --- average accuracy, statistical significance, how the errors move over time, and how well the stress warning actually works.

Two findings run through everything that follows. First, the dependency process is genuinely forecastable out of sample under a strict walk-forward design. Second, almost all of that forecastability comes from the target's own persistence, not from any new cross-asset signal. The two are not in tension --- together they pin down exactly what this forecasting problem is, and what it is not.

The top three --- Ridge, AR1, and HAR --- land almost on top of each other, with average RMSE of 0.0656, 0.0659, and 0.0659 and $R^2$ all above 0.9424. The 0.0003 spread between them is economically meaningless. Naive persistence sits just outside at 0.0666, which already tells you serial memory is doing most of the work. ElasticNet and the adaptive Ensemble form a coherent second tier above $R^2 = 0.93$. The tree methods --- GBM, Random Forest, and XGBoost --- trail well behind, with RMSE from roughly 0.09 to 0.12 and $R^2$ under 0.90. DCC-GARCH finishes last by a wide margin ($R^2 = 0.3715$).

The top cluster deserves a second look. Ridge, AR1, and HAR reach the same answer by very different routes: AR1 leans on a single autoregressive coefficient, HAR on three time scales (daily, weekly, monthly), Ridge on the full regularised feature set anchored by the latest lag. They converge because they are all chasing the same thing --- the target's strong persistence --- and the extra machinery in HAR and Ridge simply does not find anything a single lag has not already captured. That is not the complex models failing; it is the problem telling you what it is made of.

Figure~\ref{fig:sp500_forecast} shows the out-of-sample forecast panel for Bitcoin--S\&P 500 at the 30-day window. It is the natural representative case: of all the pairs, this one ties the crypto market to the broadest gauge of U.S. equity risk.

Three features of the problem stand out in the figure. The target moves smoothly, as its overlapping-window construction guarantees. The best models follow the broad trajectory with little systematic bias. The hard part is the turns: when dependence shifts abruptly, every model underreacts, just by different amounts. This is precisely where DCC-GARCH lags worst, and where machine learning can claw back a relative edge --- even while it never matches plain persistence on unconditional accuracy.

\subsection{Forecasting performance by asset class}

The averages hide some useful cross-sectional variation. Bitcoin--Ethereum is the easiest pair to forecast: the two share liquidity and sentiment drivers, so their correlation is especially smooth and autocorrelated. It posts the highest out-of-sample $R^2$ of any pair, and longer windows push it higher still.

The precious-metals pairs --- Bitcoin--Gold and Bitcoin--Silver --- sit in the middle. Their rolling correlation is regime-dependent, drifting between near-zero and weakly positive or negative. Even so, the target stays forecastable, and persistence-based models keep the lead.

The equity-index pairs, Bitcoin--S\&P 500 and Bitcoin--NASDAQ, matter most in practice. They link crypto dynamics to broad risk appetite, and among the non-crypto pairs they are the most forecastable on average: in calm regimes the dependency is smooth and strongly autocorrelated, which again favours persistence. But they are also the most regime-sensitive --- co-movement strengthens in risk-on phases and can amplify or flip violently in a dislocation. Those abrupt turns throw up episodic spikes in out-of-sample error that no model here fully catches, and they are the main short-term obstacle for equity-pair forecasting.

The Bitcoin--dollar-index pair has the weakest and most idiosyncratic dependency of the lot, and the lowest average forecastability. It still earns its place: it tests whether the crypto feature set can reach the macro-defensive channel at all.

To check whether the lower errors on equity pairs are a real advantage rather than chance, the pooled out-of-sample losses of the equity pairs (S\&P~500, NASDAQ) are tested against those of the non-equity pairs (gold, silver, dollar index) with a two-sample Diebold--Mariano test \cite{diebold1995}. This cross-group test backs up the within-experiment DM analysis of Section~4.5 and puts the forecastability differences above on firmer footing.

\subsection{The persistence dominance result}

That simple persistence models top the average ranking should be read as a diagnosis of the target, not an indictment of machine learning.

It follows directly from how the target is built. A rolling Pearson correlation over $w$ trading days shares $w-1$ observations with the previous window, which smooths the series like a moving average. After the Fisher transform, the result is strongly autocorrelated, and the first lag already accounts for the overwhelming share of predictable variance. So any model that leans on the most recent value will hit a high out-of-sample $R^2$ almost by construction.

The Random Forest feature importances say the same thing. The lagged dependency term dominates the ranking in every experiment, while the return lags, rolling volatilities, and the regime gap barely register. The problem is forecastable mainly because the target remembers its past --- not because the features hide a strong cross-asset signal independent of recent dependency.

\subsection{Comparison with DCC-GARCH}

Even though persistence baselines win the unconditional ranking, machine learning still beats DCC-GARCH by a margin that is both practically and statistically meaningful. Figure~\ref{fig:dm_heatmap} shows the Diebold--Mariano comparison between the best machine-learning model and the DCC benchmark across every experiment.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{../outputs/figures/dm_heatmap_Ridge_vs_DCC.png}
    \caption{Diebold--Mariano test results comparing Ridge (best overall RMSE across all models) against DCC-GARCH across all asset-pair and window-length combinations. Darker cells indicate stronger and more statistically significant evidence of Ridge superiority.}
    \label{fig:dm_heatmap}
\end{figure}

The DM results confirm that the best machine-learning model beats DCC-GARCH, significantly, in most experiments. There is a clear reason for it. DCC-GARCH is built to model the full conditional covariance of a return system; the rolling Pearson correlation is only a smoothed by-product of that, not the thing it optimises. The machine-learning models are trained directly on the Fisher-transformed correlation target --- they are aimed at exactly the quantity being scored, and that alignment is the advantage.

It is worth being honest about a measurement mismatch built into this comparison. The rolling Pearson target is smoothed and backward-looking, its persistence manufactured by the overlapping windows. DCC-GARCH models the instantaneous conditional covariance --- a parametric, forward-looking object --- and projecting that onto rolling Pearson space introduces a systematic lag that is a definitional incompatibility, not a model failure \cite{engle2001theoretical, andersen2003realized}. This does not void the comparison --- both are legitimate measures of dependence --- but it means the size of the gap should be read with some caution, not as a blanket verdict against DCC-GARCH.

Throughout, the thesis keeps two claims apart: \emph{benchmark superiority}, beating DCC-GARCH, and \emph{unconditional superiority}, beating AR1. Machine learning manages the first consistently and the second not at all. Both facts are informative, and it is their coexistence that defines what machine learning actually contributes here.

\subsection{Error-profile diagnostics}

Average errors are necessary but not enough; to see \emph{when} and \emph{how} a model fails you have to look at the error over time and across its distribution. Figure~\ref{fig:rolling_rmse} plots the rolling RMSE for the representative Bitcoin--S\&P 500 experiment.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{../outputs/figures/rolling_rmse_corr_BTC-USD_^GSPC_w30_fisher_z.png}
    \caption{Rolling out-of-sample RMSE over time for the Bitcoin--S\&P 500 dependency experiment. Elevated error episodes correspond to periods of abrupt regime transition in the dependency process.}
    \label{fig:rolling_rmse}
\end{figure}

Forecast quality is far from constant over time. When dependence drifts gradually, every model does well --- the persistence signal is easy for any reasonable specification to ride. When the regime breaks, error rises across the board, but by different amounts and for different lengths of time. The machine-learning models tend to recover a little faster, which fits their greater freedom to re-learn the feature--target relationship after a structural change.

The error distribution confirms that the damage comes from episodic underreaction at sharp turning points, not from any standing bias. The models track the slow, persistent part of the dependency reliably and stumble on the fast, discontinuous part. That asymmetry matters directly for the signal layer, whose whole job is to catch the onset of stress in time.

To make this regime-sensitivity concrete, Appendix~\ref{app:market_events} walks through eleven landmark events from 2020 to 2026 --- from the COVID crash of March 2020 and the FTX collapse of November 2022 to the Bitcoin ETF approval of January 2024 and the tariff shock of April 2025. Each gets a four-panel diagnostic of how the rolling BTC correlation moved around the event and its pre-event baseline, with links to the original reporting. The case studies line up with the main finding: the biggest errors in Figure~\ref{fig:rolling_rmse} fall on the sharpest events in the record.

\subsection{Investor signal layer: average results}

The second layer turns the dependency forecast into a stress-warning classifier. Table~\ref{tab:signal_summary} reports each classifier family's average performance across all asset pairs and windows.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{Average investor-signal performance by classifier family, aggregated across all experiments.}
    \label{tab:signal_summary}
    \begin{tabular}{lrrr}
        \toprule
        Signal model & Balanced Accuracy & F1\_down & AUC \\
        \midrule
        Logit    & 0.511 & 0.214 & 0.513 \\
        GBM\_Cls & 0.508 & 0.158 & 0.517 \\
        RF\_Cls  & 0.502 & 0.042 & 0.519 \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \par\smallskip\noindent\footnotesize{\textit{Note: Results averaged across all asset pairs and window lengths. Balanced Accuracy corrects for class imbalance. F1\textsubscript{down} measures detection quality for the stress class only.}}
\end{table}

Classification is a much harder problem than the regression layer. Stress days are noisy, rare, and costly to miss in an asymmetric way. The logistic classifier holds the highest F1 on the stress class (0.214 on average), meaning it stays sensitive to real stress without collapsing into a do-nothing, low-exit-rate state. GBM reaches a similar Balanced Accuracy (0.508) but a lower F1 (0.158): it concentrates its probability mass and fires less often. Random Forest falls apart, with F1 near zero (0.042) --- a degenerate, barely-fires classifier that is useless in practice despite a respectable-looking AUC.

That near-zero F1 is worth a closer look. Random Forest's predicted probabilities bunch into a narrow band, so moving the threshold barely changes anything; GBM, by contrast, manages a modest but real F1 (0.158). Two things compound to break Random Forest here: the severe imbalance between stress and non-stress days, and the lack of probability calibration --- ensemble methods are notorious for poorly calibrated probabilities unless you post-process them with Platt scaling or isotonic regression \cite{lopezdeprado2018advances}. Calibration should be a mandatory step before threshold-based signal extraction in any follow-up work.

None of this is surprising: in low-signal-to-noise classification with correlated predictors, a regularised linear boundary usually generalises better out of sample than a high-capacity ensemble that pays for its flexibility in variance.

\subsection{Best signal configurations}

The best single Logit result is on Bitcoin--S\&P 500. At the 30-day window it reaches a Balanced Accuracy of 0.5308 and an F1 of 0.2430 on the downside class --- a modest but genuine step above the uninformative 0.50 baseline, and one that holds up statistically.

Figure~\ref{fig:signal_sp500} presents the signal output for this configuration, illustrating the temporal distribution of predicted stress flags relative to realized stress events.

    \caption{Investor stress-warning signal for the Bitcoin--S\&P 500 pair at the 30-day window. The upper panel displays the predicted stress probability from the logistic classifier together with the decision threshold; the lower panel marks flagged and missed stress events.}

The signal is meant to work as a probability-guided warning that flags elevated-risk periods and supports a risk-reduction decision, not a directional trade. On NASDAQ it is noticeably weaker: Logit Balanced Accuracy for Bitcoin--NASDAQ drops below 0.50 at the two longer windows (60 and 90 days) and averages 0.4997 across all four, the worst of any pair in the signal layer. The contrast with the S\&P~500 makes economic sense --- the NASDAQ Composite leans heavily on growth-sensitive technology names whose stress is less tightly bound to global risk appetite than the broader index. For precious metals and the dollar proxy, results are mixed but mostly above chance at the shorter windows, which fits the idea that crypto carries some, but weaker, information about stress in those markets.

\subsection{Practical interpretation of signal-layer performance}

The signal's usefulness only means something against a stated decision rule. An investor using it acts selectively, not every day. It informs a conditional call --- whether to trim exposure, tighten stops, or hedge harder when the stress probability rises.

Seen this way, the exit rate --- how often the signal is switched on --- is as important as accuracy. A signal that never fires is useless no matter how accurate it looks on paper; one that fires constantly just runs up trading costs and dilutes its own content. The logistic model strikes a defensible balance: active often enough to be useful, not so often that it churns.

\subsection{Sensitivity to rolling window length}

Window length is a first-order choice, and it visibly shapes both the target and the results. Short windows give responsive but noisy estimates; long windows give smooth, heavily autocorrelated targets that are easier to forecast but slower to register a real structural change.

The experiments point to the medium windows --- $w = 30$ days in particular --- as the sweet spot: smooth enough for high out-of-sample $R^2$, still responsive enough to be useful for stress detection. At longer windows the persistence effect only strengthens, squeezing the gap between the simple baselines and the complex models even tighter. That interaction between window length and model performance is itself a structural finding, and a useful guide for choosing the horizon in future work.

\subsection{Summary of empirical findings}

The empirical results of the thesis are summarized in four principal statements:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Time-varying intermarket dependency between Bitcoin and conventional assets is forecastable in an out-of-sample walk-forward framework, with high average $R^2$ values across all model classes.
    \item The dominant source of predictive power is the serial persistence of the rolling correlation target; Ridge, AR1, and HAR form a near-indistinguishable top cluster, all exploiting this persistence through different mechanisms, while tree-based methods trail substantially.
    \item Machine-learning models frequently and significantly outperform the leakage-safe DCC-GARCH benchmark; Ridge achieves the best overall average RMSE (0.0656) across all 24 experiments.
    \item The investor signal layer delivers moderate, statistically non-trivial classification results that are most pronounced for broad equity markets and are best interpreted as a supplementary risk-management input rather than a self-contained directional predictor.
\end{enumerate}

These findings are consistent with one another and, together, enough to carry both the scientific and the applied contributions of the thesis. The next section takes up what they mean more broadly.

Put together, the evidence paints a coherent picture of how crypto information feeds into conventional-asset risk. The channel is real and measurable, but its predictive content lives mostly in the momentum of the dependency series itself, not in the nonlinear capacity of gradient-boosted or random-forest models. In practice the dependency is best thought of as a slowly evolving, regime-sensitive quantity that rewards smooth, low-complexity rules over flexible high-capacity ones. Chapter~\ref{sec:discussion} develops this reading and places it in the wider literature on intermarket contagion and financial machine learning.
